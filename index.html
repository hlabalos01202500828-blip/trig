📐 Angles & Radian Measure

CORE CONCEPTS

What is an Angle?

An angle is formed by rotating a ray from an initial side to a terminal side about a vertex. Measured in degrees or radians.

  • Positive angle → counterclockwise rotation
  • Negative angle → clockwise rotation

Standard Position

An angle is in standard position when:

  • Vertex is at the origin
  • Initial side is along the positive x-axis

This is the default setup for all trig work.

🔄 Degrees vs Radians

Conversion Formulas: To convert DEGREES → RADIANS: multiply by π/180 To convert RADIANS → DEGREES: multiply by 180/π Key Relationship: 360° = 2π radians | 180° = π radians | 90° = π/2 radians

Radians are preferred in engineering and physics because they make calculus cleaner (derivatives of sin/cos don't need extra constants).

🔗 Coterminal Angles

Coterminal angles share the same terminal side. Add or subtract multiples of 360° (or 2π).

Coterminal angle = θ ± 360°·n (degrees) Coterminal angle = θ ± 2π·n (radians) where n is any integer
📌 Example: Find two coterminal angles for 50°

Add 360°

50° + 360° = 410°

Subtract 360°

50° − 360° = −310°

All three angles (50°, 410°, −310°) point to the same terminal side.

🎯 Reference Angles

The reference angle θ' is the acute positive angle between the terminal side and the x-axis. Always between 0° and 90°.

Finding Reference Angles: Q1: θ' = θ Q2: θ' = 180° − θ Q3: θ' = θ − 180° Q4: θ' = 360° − θ
📌 Example: Find the reference angle for 235°

Identify the quadrant

235° is between 180° and 270° → Quadrant III

Apply Q3 formula

θ' = 235° − 180° = 55°

🏹 Arc Length & Sector Area

Arc Length: s = rθ (θ must be in RADIANS!) Sector Area: A = ½r²θ (θ must be in RADIANS!) Angular Velocity: ω = θ/t (radians per second)
📌 Example: A wheel with radius 5 m rotates 120°. Find arc length.

Convert to radians

120° × π/180 = 2π/3 rad

Apply formula

s = rθ = 5 × 2π/3 = 10π/3 ≈ 10.47 m

Engineering tip: Physics and calculus always use radians. Train yourself to think in radians. π/2 = right angle, π = straight line.

⭕ The Unit Circle

What is the Unit Circle?

A circle centered at the origin with radius r = 1. For any angle θ, the point on the circle is (cos θ, sin θ). This is the foundation of all trigonometry.

The equation: x² + y² = 1

🧠 How to Remember the Values

The "1-2-3" Trick for sin values in Q1:

sin 0° = √0/2 = 0 sin 30° = √1/2 = 1/2 sin 45° = √2/2 ≈ 0.707 sin 60° = √3/2 ≈ 0.866 sin 90° = √4/2 = 1 The numerators go: √0, √1, √2, √3, √4 — just count 0,1,2,3,4 under the radical!

For cosine, it's the same values in reverse order: cos 0°=1, cos 30°=√3/2, cos 45°=√2/2, cos 60°=1/2, cos 90°=0

🔺 The Six Trig Functions

📐 Right Triangle Definitions (SOH-CAH-TOA)

Given a right triangle with angle θ, hypotenuse h, opposite side o, adjacent side a: PRIMARY: sin θ = opp / hyp cos θ = adj / hyp tan θ = opp / adj RECIPROCAL: csc θ = hyp / opp sec θ = hyp / adj cot θ = adj / opp Memory: SOH-CAH-TOA S = Sin, O = Opp, H = Hyp C = Cos, A = Adj, H = Hyp T = Tan, O = Opp, A = Adj
Reciprocal pairs: sin↔csc, cos↔sec, tan↔cot. If you know the "big three" (sin, cos, tan), you can always find the other three.

🟢 Quadrant Signs — ASTC Rule

Remember: "All Students Take Calculus" — tells you which functions are POSITIVE in each quadrant.

QUADRANT II (90°-180°)

A = All | S = Sin | T = Tan | C = Cos → reads counterclockwise from Q1.

🔢 Exact Values — Practice These Until Automatic

📌 Example 1: Find all 6 trig functions of θ = 30°

For a 30-60-90 triangle: sides are 1, √3, 2 (opposite 30°, 60°, 90°)

sin 30° = 1/2 csc 30° = 2 cos 30° = √3/2 sec 30° = 2/√3 = 2√3/3 tan 30° = 1/√3 cot 30° = √3
📌 Example 2: sin θ = 3/5 in Quadrant II. Find cos θ and tan θ.

Use Pythagorean identity

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 → (3/5)² + cos²θ = 1 → cos²θ = 1 − 9/25 = 16/25

Apply quadrant sign rule

In Q2, cos is NEGATIVE. So cos θ = −4/5

Find tan

tan θ = sin/cos = (3/5)/(−4/5) = −3/4

📐 Special Right Triangles

30-60-90 Triangle

Sides in ratio: 1 : √3 : 2 (opposite 30°, 60°, 90°) If hypotenuse = 2: Short leg = 1 (opp 30°) Long leg = √3 (opp 60°)

45-45-90 Triangle

Sides in ratio: 1 : 1 : √2 (legs equal, hyp = leg × √2) If legs = 1: Hypotenuse = √2 sin 45° = cos 45° = √2/2

🔗 Reciprocal & Quotient Relationships

csc θ = 1/sin θ sec θ = 1/cos θ cot θ = 1/tan θ tan θ = sin θ / cos θ cot θ = cos θ / sin θ

🔑 Trigonometric Identities

Identities are equations that are true for all values of the variable. They are used to simplify expressions, solve equations, and prove other identities. These are the backbone of all advanced trig work.

🧱 The Fundamental Identities

PYTHAGOREAN IDENTITIES (memorize all 3): sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 ← most important 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ ← divide first by cos²θ 1 + cot²θ = csc²θ ← divide first by sin²θ RECIPROCAL IDENTITIES: csc θ = 1/sin θ sec θ = 1/cos θ cot θ = 1/tan θ QUOTIENT IDENTITIES: tan θ = sin θ/cos θ cot θ = cos θ/sin θ EVEN/ODD IDENTITIES: cos(−θ) = cos θ [cosine is EVEN] sin(−θ) = −sin θ [sine is ODD] tan(−θ) = −tan θ [tangent is ODD] CO-FUNCTION IDENTITIES: sin θ = cos(90°−θ) cos θ = sin(90°−θ) tan θ = cot(90°−θ) sec θ = csc(90°−θ)

✍️ Proving Identities — Strategy Guide

Work ONE side only Convert to sin/cos first Use Pythagorean ID Factor when possible Work the complex side
📌 Prove: tan²θ + 1 = sec²θ

Start with LHS: tan²θ + 1

Convert tan to sin/cos: (sin θ/cos θ)² + 1

Simplify

= sin²θ/cos²θ + cos²θ/cos²θ = (sin²θ + cos²θ)/cos²θ

Apply Pythagorean identity

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, so = 1/cos²θ

Rewrite as reciprocal

= (1/cos θ)² = sec²θ ✓

📌 Prove: sin θ · sec θ = tan θ

LHS: sin θ · sec θ

Replace sec θ = 1/cos θ

Multiply

= sin θ · (1/cos θ) = sin θ/cos θ

Recognize quotient identity

= tan θ ✓

📌 Prove: (1 − cos²θ)/sin θ = sin θ

Numerator: 1 − cos²θ

From sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, we get 1 − cos²θ = sin²θ

Substitute

= sin²θ / sin θ

Simplify

= sin θ ✓

When stuck proving an identity: (1) write everything in terms of sin and cos, (2) look for Pythagorean identity substitutions, (3) try factoring or multiplying by a clever form of 1.
NEVER cross-multiply across the equals sign or move terms from one side to the other when proving identities. You must only manipulate one side at a time.

🔄 Inverse Trig Functions

Inverse trig functions "undo" trig functions. Written as arcsin, arccos, arctan (or sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹).

arcsin x = sin⁻¹(x) returns angle whose sin = x arccos x = cos⁻¹(x) returns angle whose cos = x arctan x = tan⁻¹(x) returns angle whose tan = x Restricted Domains (to make them functions): arcsin: domain [−1,1], range [−π/2, π/2] arccos: domain [−1,1], range [0, π] arctan: domain all reals, range (−π/2, π/2)
arctan is used CONSTANTLY in engineering — finding angles from force components (statics), signal phase angles (circuits), and slopes in navigation.

➕ Advanced Formulas

📐 Sum & Difference Formulas

SIN: sin(A + B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B sin(A − B) = sin A cos B − cos A sin B COS: cos(A + B) = cos A cos B − sin A sin B cos(A − B) = cos A cos B + sin A sin B TAN: tan(A + B) = (tan A + tan B) / (1 − tan A tan B) tan(A − B) = (tan A − tan B) / (1 + tan A tan B) MEMORY TIP for cos: the sign FLIPS (+ becomes −, − becomes +) For sin: the sign STAYS the same
📌 Find the exact value of sin(75°)

Write 75° as sum of known angles

75° = 45° + 30°

Apply sin sum formula

sin(45° + 30°) = sin 45° cos 30° + cos 45° sin 30°

Substitute exact values

= (√2/2)(√3/2) + (√2/2)(1/2) = √6/4 + √2/4

Final answer

= (√6 + √2)/4

2️⃣ Double Angle Formulas

SIN: sin(2A) = 2 sin A cos A COS (3 versions — all equal!): cos(2A) = cos²A − sin²A cos(2A) = 2cos²A − 1 cos(2A) = 1 − 2sin²A TAN: tan(2A) = 2 tan A / (1 − tan²A)
📌 If sin θ = 3/5 (Q1), find sin(2θ) and cos(2θ)

Find cos θ first

cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ = 1 − 9/25 = 16/25. In Q1, cos θ = 4/5

Apply sin double angle

sin(2θ) = 2(3/5)(4/5) = 24/25

Apply cos double angle

cos(2θ) = cos²θ − sin²θ = 16/25 − 9/25 = 7/25

sin(2θ) = 24/25, cos(2θ) = 7/25

½ Half Angle Formulas

sin(A/2) = ± √[(1 − cos A) / 2] cos(A/2) = ± √[(1 + cos A) / 2] tan(A/2) = ± √[(1 − cos A) / (1 + cos A)] = sin A / (1 + cos A) = (1 − cos A) / sin A The ± depends on which quadrant A/2 is in!
📌 Find sin(22.5°) exactly using half-angle formula

Recognize 22.5° = 45°/2

Use sin(A/2) formula with A = 45°

Look up cos 45°

cos 45° = √2/2

Apply formula

sin(22.5°) = √[(1 − √2/2)/2] = √[(2−√2)/4] = √(2−√2)/2

Sign check

22.5° is in Q1, so + applies. Answer: √(2−√2)/2 ≈ 0.383

⚡ Power-Reducing Formulas (Key for Physics!)

sin²A = (1 − cos 2A) / 2 cos²A = (1 + cos 2A) / 2 tan²A = (1 − cos 2A) / (1 + cos 2A) Used in: AC circuit power calculations, RMS values, integration in calculus

🔗 Product-to-Sum & Sum-to-Product

Product → Sum: sin A cos B = ½[sin(A+B) + sin(A−B)] cos A sin B = ½[sin(A+B) − sin(A−B)] cos A cos B = ½[cos(A+B) + cos(A−B)] sin A sin B = ½[cos(A−B) − cos(A+B)] Sum → Product: sin A + sin B = 2 sin[(A+B)/2] cos[(A−B)/2] sin A − sin B = 2 cos[(A+B)/2] sin[(A−B)/2] cos A + cos B = 2 cos[(A+B)/2] cos[(A−B)/2] cos A − cos B = −2 sin[(A+B)/2] sin[(A−B)/2]
These are huge in signal processing and Fourier analysis — you'll use them in ECE/EEE circuits and communication engineering!

⚖️ Law of Sines & Law of Cosines

Use these when you have a non-right triangle (oblique triangles). They're fundamental in surveying, navigation, structural engineering, and statics.

📐 Law of Sines

a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C Or equivalently: sin A/a = sin B/b = sin C/c where a, b, c are sides opposite to angles A, B, C respectively

Use Law of Sines when you have:

  • AAS — two angles + one side
  • ASA — two angles + included side
  • SSA — two sides + non-included angle (⚠️ ambiguous case!)
SSA (Ambiguous Case): Depending on the values, there may be 0, 1, or 2 valid triangles. Always check! Given angle A and sides a, b: if a < b·sin A → 0 triangles; if a = b·sin A → 1 triangle; if a > b → 1 triangle; if b·sin A < a < b → 2 triangles.
📌 In triangle ABC, A = 35°, B = 65°, a = 8. Find b.

Set up the ratio

a/sin A = b/sin B → 8/sin 35° = b/sin 65°

Solve for b

b = 8 · sin 65°/sin 35° = 8 × 0.9063/0.5736

Calculate

b = ≈ 12.64

📐 Law of Cosines

a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A b² = a² + c² − 2ac cos B c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C Note: If A = 90°, cos A = 0, reduces to Pythagorean theorem! Solving for angles: cos A = (b² + c² − a²) / (2bc)

Use Law of Cosines when you have:

  • SAS — two sides + included angle
  • SSS — all three sides given
📌 In triangle ABC: a = 7, b = 10, C = 50°. Find c.

Identify what's given

SAS: sides a=7, b=10, included angle C=50°

Apply Law of Cosines

c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C = 49 + 100 − 2(7)(10)cos 50°

Compute

= 149 − 140(0.6428) = 149 − 89.99 = 59.01

Take square root

c = √59.01 ≈ 7.68

📐 Area Formulas

Standard area: A = ½ bh (base × height) Using trig (SAS): A = ½ ab sin C Heron's formula (SSS): s = (a + b + c)/2 [semi-perimeter] A = √[s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)]
📌 Find area: a = 5, b = 8, C = 45°

A = ½ × 5 × 8 × sin 45° = 20 × (√2/2) = 10√2 ≈ 14.14 sq units

📋 General Solution Templates

If sin θ = k: θ = arcsin(k) + 2πn θ = π − arcsin(k) + 2πn If cos θ = k: θ = ±arccos(k) + 2πn If tan θ = k: θ = arctan(k) + πn (only need ONE family of solutions!)
📌 Solve: 2 sin θ − 1 = 0 for 0 ≤ θ < 2π

Isolate sin θ

2 sin θ = 1 → sin θ = 1/2

Find reference angle

arcsin(1/2) = π/6 (30°)

Apply ASTC — sin is + in Q1 and Q2

Q1: θ = π/6     Q2: θ = π − π/6 = 5π/6

Final answer

θ = π/6 and θ = 5π/6

📌 Solve: tan²θ − 3 = 0 for 0 ≤ θ < 2π

Isolate tan²θ

tan²θ = 3 → tan θ = ±√3

For tan θ = +√3

Reference angle: arctan(√3) = π/3 (60°). Tan is + in Q1 and Q3: θ = π/3, 4π/3

For tan θ = −√3

Tan is − in Q2 and Q4: θ = 2π/3, 5π/3

All solutions

θ = π/3, 2π/3, 4π/3, 5π/3

📌 Solve: 2 cos²θ + cos θ − 1 = 0 for 0 ≤ θ < 2π

Factor as quadratic (let u = cos θ)

2u² + u − 1 = (2u − 1)(u + 1) = 0

Solve each factor

2u − 1 = 0 → cos θ = 1/2     u + 1 = 0 → cos θ = −1

For cos θ = 1/2

Cos is + in Q1 and Q4: θ = π/3, 5π/3

For cos θ = −1

θ = π

All solutions

θ = π/3, π, 5π/3

Common technique: convert everything to one trig function using identities, then treat as a polynomial equation. Factor, apply zero-product property, solve each.

⚡ Physics & Engineering Applications

🎯 Vectors & Force Components

Any vector (force, velocity, acceleration) can be split into x and y components using trig. This is the most-used skill in statics and dynamics.

Given vector magnitude |F| at angle θ from horizontal: F_x = |F| cos θ (horizontal component) F_y = |F| sin θ (vertical component) Reverse: Given components: |F| = √(F_x² + F_y²) (magnitude) θ = arctan(F_y / F_x) (direction)
📌 A force of 100 N acts at 37° above horizontal. Find its components.

Horizontal (x) component

F_x = 100 cos 37° = 100 × 0.7986 ≈ 79.86 N

Vertical (y) component

F_y = 100 sin 37° = 100 × 0.6018 ≈ 60.18 N

Verify

√(79.86² + 60.18²) ≈ √(9999) ≈ 100 N ✓

🚀 Projectile Motion

Initial velocity components (angle θ with horizontal): v₀ₓ = v₀ cos θ (constant horizontal velocity) v₀ᵧ = v₀ sin θ (initial vertical velocity) Position at time t: x(t) = v₀ cos θ · t y(t) = v₀ sin θ · t − ½g t² Maximum height: H = (v₀ sin θ)² / (2g) Range (horizontal distance): R = v₀² sin(2θ) / g Maximum range occurs at: θ = 45° Time of flight: T = 2v₀ sin θ / g
📌 A ball is thrown at 20 m/s at 60°. Find: max height, range, time of flight. (g = 9.8 m/s²)

Initial components

v₀ₓ = 20 cos 60° = 10 m/s    v₀ᵧ = 20 sin 60° = 17.32 m/s

Max height

H = v₀ᵧ²/(2g) = (17.32)²/(2×9.8) = 300/(19.6) ≈ 15.31 m

Time of flight

T = 2v₀ sin 60°/g = 2(20)(0.866)/9.8 ≈ 3.53 s

Range

R = v₀² sin(2×60°)/g = 400 sin120°/9.8 = 400(0.866)/9.8 ≈ 35.4 m

🌊 Simple Harmonic Motion & Waves

Position: x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) or A sin(ωt + φ) where: A = amplitude (maximum displacement) ω = angular frequency (rad/s) = 2πf = 2π/T f = frequency (Hz) T = period (seconds) = 1/f φ = phase angle (initial offset) Wave equation: y = A sin(kx − ωt) k = wave number = 2π/λ λ = wavelength
In electrical engineering: voltage and current in AC circuits are sinusoidal. V(t) = Vₘ sin(ωt + φ), where φ is the phase shift. This leads directly to phasors and impedance — all trig!

⚡ AC Circuits (Preview for ECE/EEE)

Phasor representation: V = Vₘ∠θ = Vₘ(cos θ + j sin θ) (This is Euler's formula — trig meets complex numbers) Impedance: Z = R + jX |Z| = √(R² + X²) (magnitude using Pythagorean theorem!) φ = arctan(X/R) (phase angle using arctan!) Power factor = cos φ (cosine appears in power calculations!)

🏗️ Statics — Equilibrium

For static equilibrium (sum of forces = 0): ΣF_x = 0 → Σ(F cos θ) = 0 ΣF_y = 0 → Σ(F sin θ) = 0 For inclined plane problems: Weight component along slope: W sin θ Weight component perpendicular: W cos θ Normal force: N = W cos θ Friction force: f = μN = μW cos θ
📌 A 50 kg box sits on a 30° incline. Find the normal force and force needed to hold it.

Weight

W = mg = 50 × 9.8 = 490 N

Normal force (perpendicular to slope)

N = W cos 30° = 490 × (√3/2) ≈ 424.4 N

Force along slope (to hold it)

F = W sin 30° = 490 × 0.5 = 245 N
